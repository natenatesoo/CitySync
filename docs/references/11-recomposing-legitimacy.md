## Full Text

### Why Public Institutions Be Sticky

Every project seeking to reform government eventually runs into the same brick wall: governments do not change simply because better ideas exist. We live in a world where institutions are not optimized for innovation — they are optimized for stability and legitimacy under constraint. That's why cities keep running on legacy IT systems that should have been retired decades ago, and why bureaucracies cling to processes that frustrate nearly everyone involved. Their first mission is to reduce risk, not to pioneer novelty.

The economist Douglass North famously described institutions as the "rules of the game," with organizations playing as the "teams." These rules endure because they reduce uncertainty. Everyone knows where they stand, and if you change the rules mid-game, you change the payoff structures for every actor — suddenly even people who support your reform in principle may resist it in practice. This is the paradox that makes reform so difficult. Uncertainty is expensive, and public agencies are structurally designed to avoid it.

Public choice theory helps us see another layer of the problem. Organized interests — whether unions, contractors, or vendors — are highly motivated to defend favorable rules. Meanwhile, the public, who may bear the costs of inefficiency or dysfunction, experiences them in diffuse ways, causing them to remain poorly mobilized. The result is a lopsided battlefield where concentrated, organized defenders of the status quo face off against the dispersed and fragmented advocates of change. If your reform threatens a revenue stream, jurisdictional sovereignty, or a bargaining chip, you can expect a sophisticated and sustained pushback, even when the public benefit is obvious.

Then there's the survival logic of bureaucracy itself. Leaders inside agencies want budget stability, the clarity of their mandate, and above all, the avoidance of scandal. One failed IT upgrade can end a career, while maintaining a decades-old system rarely carries the same reputational risk. That's why large IT shifts, even when obviously necessary, are avoided. Agencies prefer incrementalism over transformation, even when incrementalism itself becomes the larger long-term risk.

This principle also applies to the world of procurement — the chokepoint through which all administrative reform must pass. Procurement represents one of the largest markets in the world, accounting for around 10–15% of GDP within OECD countries. The rules within this realm are designed to prevent favoritism and corruption, but in practice they privilege incumbents. The thresholds for qualification, requirements for past performance, insurance and bonding clauses, and contracting vehicles are all optimized for scale and tilt the field away from newcomers. A superior product loses if it cannot navigate procurement law.

When you stack these layers together — statutes, budget cycles, unions, auditors, legacy IT, and procurement rules — you start to see why institutional reform is so sticky. And this is before you even add in the cultural weight of bureaucracy itself. These systems favor compliance over experimentation. They prefer it when pilot projects can't scale. Over the past few decades, governments have cultivated a culture that punishes failed experimentation but tolerates a costly status quo.

So, the challenge is not just technical. It is institutional. The obvious question becomes: how do you "defeat" incumbents ethically? How can reformers like us, working through projects like City/Sync, actually move the immovable?

### The Theory of Change: Recomposition, Not Disruption

**Step 1 — Reframe the objective.** The word "innovation" is often a red flag in public administration, signaling risk and volatility. The language that works is "risk management." The truth is that legacy systems already pose enormous risks — continuity risks when the last COBOL programmer retires, procurement risks when cities are locked into a single vendor with escalating costs, governance risks when records are opaque and unauditable. If City/Sync can show that it reduces those risks with audit trails, rollback capacity, and verifiable compliance, then it is not introducing risk but removing it.

**Step 2 — Avoid "big-bang" replacement.** We need a parallel substrate — a connective backbone that can run alongside existing systems and gradually take on more responsibility. Estonia's X-Road project offers the best template: instead of replacing systems, X-Road created a data exchange layer that allowed agencies to interoperate without losing control of their own databases. Over time, this became the backbone of Estonia's digital state. City/Sync's "local chain" can function similarly — not as a new app or a single portal, but as an auditable, city-sovereign layer for decisions, records, processes, and rules.

**Step 3 — Borrow from participatory governance experiments.** vTaiwan pioneered a hybrid online/offline process for deliberation, using tools like Polis to cluster opinions and identify consensus. Decidim, launched in Barcelona, went further by shipping not just software but a governance contract alongside it, ensuring that participation was legitimate and binding. Both show how to build processes that give public officials political cover and citizens real input. If City/Sync can institutionalize new methods for deliberation and collective action into its dApps, upgrades become not just technical but democratic and backed by incumbent authority.

**Step 4 — Use regulatory sandboxes and challenge-based procurement.** Challenge.gov has shown that buying outcomes instead of predefined products allows novel vendors to compete fairly. These models can be replicated at the municipal level and trialed under controlled conditions before they scale.

**Step 5 — Make procurement part of the product.** If we don't hand vendors a compliant procurement pathway with ready-made statements of work, evaluation criteria, and privacy addenda, we will fail before we begin. A city official who can copy-paste CitySync documents into a council packet without months of legal review is far more likely to move forward with these experiments.

**Step 6 — Start with high-visibility, low-risk workflows.** City/Sync should not start with contested systems like payroll or benefits disbursement. It should start with workflows that are high-visibility but low-risk: special event permits, micro-grants, participatory budgeting, volunteerism — processes where citizens can quickly engage and public officials can claim credit. From there, momentum builds.

**Step 7 — Build the full coalition.** Council-members for agenda-setting, clerks for records, auditors for oversight, unions for job security, civil society for legitimacy. Without them, resistance mounts in unforeseen ways. With them, replacement becomes feasible. It's not enough to have code — we need to be embedded in governance and integrated into the process.

**Step 8 — Bank legitimacy, then target core systems.** Once auditors bless the logs, public officials claim credit for improved outcomes, and regulators legally recognize parallel systems, CitySync can expand to core systems: municipal/vendor payments, the evolution of $CITY issuance, and multi-agency dPANs targeting more complicated collective action problems.

**Success metrics.** Success should be measured not just in adoption but in the things that matter to residents: service times, state/local documentation, response speed, equitable access across neighborhoods, and broader participation rates in public-service provision. City/Sync can build cryptographic proofs into the logs to offer verifiable assurance that the numbers are real.

This is a theory of change rooted not in disruption but in **recomposition**. We aren't trying to blow up government. We are trying to recompose it into smaller, verifiable, participatory systems that can evolve over time. The goal is to edit the rules of the game without stopping play — seeking, in Ostrom's words, a polycentric governance system where many centers of decision-making balance each other, making the whole more resilient.

Public institutions are sticky because they were built to be. But stickiness is not permanent. If we can out-coordinate the status quo and prove safety to auditors, legitimacy to citizens, compliance to regulators, and offer political capital to public officials, we can shift the gravitational pull away from the power centers of administrative authority.

And if we get that right, the very thing that once made reform impossible — the stickiness of institutions — becomes our greatest ally. Because once the new system is in place, it will be just as hard to undo as the old one ever was.

### So, Why Local Chains?

If the stickiness of public institutions is our starting point, then the design choices behind City/Sync must be judged not by their technical ease but by their institutional plausibility. This is where the decision to prioritize local sovereign chains over applications deployed on global, permissionless networks becomes essential. The argument is not about decentralization — it is about institutional adoptability.

**1. Legal compliance.** Public institutions cannot run their core processes in environments that sit outside of their jurisdictional reach. Archiving, records retention, freedom of information requirements, and GDPR-style privacy protections are statutory obligations — they cannot be optional features. A city council cannot tell its auditors "the chain decided otherwise." A city-sovereign chain ensures that compliance is engineered into the substrate itself, not left as an afterthought in middleware.

**2. Security and risk framing.** Ethereum is designed to resist adversarial global threats, but that resilience comes with exposure to onchain dynamics like MEV extraction, network congestion, unintended upgrades, and spam attacks. Governments do not want to play in adversarial sandboxes. They want bounded and predictable environments. A local chain, validated by a consortium of nonprofits, agencies, and trusted civic organizations, can narrow the attack surface and transform decentralization into something auditors can understand — by placing accountability on a known validator set.

**3. Economic predictability.** Public budgets are written years in advance. No procurement officer is going to sign off on systems whose costs depend on speculative fluctuations in the price of ETH. Local chains remove this barrier by offering gasless transactions and a stable, narrow token set ($USDC for funding, $CITY for engagement, $VOTE for governance). For the first time, blockchains can speak the language of municipal finance officers — a predictable model for fixed costs with no hidden volatility.

**4. Procurement operability.** "Deploy it on Ethereum" is not a category a city can buy. It cannot be written into an RFP, nor does it define liability when things go wrong. A local chain, however, can be wrapped in ready-made procurement templates that authorize statements of work, compliance addenda, evaluation criteria, and a chain of custody through a trusted set of institutions. It gives procurement officers something tangible they can insert into a council agenda with clear guardrails for accountability. The chain itself is a contract-ready product.

**5. Political optics and trust.** Citizens will not look kindly on their governments outsourcing essential functions to a global network run by anonymous actors. Even if the technology is sound, the perception will be one of surrendering sovereignty. A local chain avoids this trap by making governance visible and proximate. Validators are local nonprofits, universities, agencies, and organizations run by people who are known and can be addressed. Your governance, your records, your rules.

**6. Interoperability on public-sector terms.** Governments do not want to be locked in silos, but neither do they want to be swallowed into global protocols they cannot influence. Local chains allow for interoperability through bridges when needed — for regional cooperation or citizen mobility — without governments losing their discretionary authority. Local chains can mirror the structure of federalism itself.

**7. Institutional resilience.** A city-specific chain, designed around its laws, validators, and governance processes, becomes resilient by design. Once adopted, it is not easily undone. This is exactly the kind of durable infrastructure reformers should want: not fragile pilot projects that fade away, but hard-to-reverse substrates that persist across political cycles.

Taken together, these arguments show that the choice of local sovereign chains is not a matter of taste. It is the only viable way to reconcile the technical promises of blockchain with the institutional realities of government. Public agencies need legal compliance, risk reduction, cost predictability, procurement clarity, political legitimacy, controlled interoperability, and lasting durability. Ethereum dApps may maximize theoretical openness, but local chains maximize practical adoptability.

**In the context of reforming public administration, adoptability is the only currency that counts.**

## Key Concepts for CitySync

- **Institutional stickiness as the starting design constraint** — Douglass North's "rules of the game" framing: institutions endure because they reduce uncertainty, and changing them shifts payoff structures for every actor. CitySync's architecture must be designed to reduce uncertainty for every stakeholder it touches (auditors, unions, procurement officers, council-members), not just to maximize decentralization.
- **Recomposition, not disruption** — the theory of change for CitySync. Not replacing government but recomposing it into smaller, verifiable, participatory systems. X-Road (parallel substrate), vTaiwan (democratic legitimacy for upgrades), Decidim (governance contracts alongside software). These are the design templates, not Ethereum dApps.
- **Risk management, not innovation** — the language that works in public administration. CitySync should be positioned as reducing existing risks (opaque records, vendor lock-in, unauditable processes) rather than introducing new ones. Every contract design decision should be evaluable through this lens: does this reduce risk or introduce it?
- **Procurement as product** — CitySync cannot succeed if it doesn't ship procurement-ready documentation alongside its contracts: statements of work, compliance addenda, evaluation criteria, privacy frameworks. This is a first-class deliverable, not an afterthought. A city official who can copy-paste into a council packet without months of legal review is a successful CitySync deployment.
- **High-visibility, low-risk entry point** — start with volunteerism, participatory budgeting, micro-grants, special event permits. These are the on-ramps. Don't start with payroll, benefits, or any contested system. Build legitimacy before claiming territory.
- **Full coalition required** — council-members (agenda), clerks (records), auditors (oversight), unions (job security), civil society (legitimacy). CitySync's pilot design should map each stakeholder to a specific governance role and a specific way of claiming credit for the system's success.
- **Bank legitimacy, then expand** — the sequencing principle. Cryptographic proofs in logs → auditor blessing → public official credit claiming → regulator recognition → expand to core systems. This is the phase-gate logic that should inform the pilot ordinance structure.
- **Seven institutional justifications for local chains** — legal compliance, security/risk framing, economic predictability, procurement operability, political optics/trust, interoperability on public-sector terms, institutional resilience. These seven arguments are the complete answer to any "why not just use Ethereum?" critique and should be part of CitySync's standard pitch materials.
- **Stickiness as ally, not enemy** — the key insight of the essay's conclusion. Once the local chain is embedded, it is just as hard to undo as the legacy system it replaces. Design for durability across political cycles, not just for the current administration's approval.
- **Adoptability over openness** — "Ethereum dApps maximize theoretical openness; local chains maximize practical adoptability. In the context of reforming public administration, adoptability is the only currency that counts." This is the definitive statement of CitySync's architecture philosophy. Every technical decision should be evaluated against adoptability, not ideological purity.
- **Sepolia pilot as institutional proof-of-concept** — the Sepolia testnet pilot is not just a technical test. It is an institutional test: can CitySync produce the audit trails, compliance documentation, and governance records that a city attorney and procurement officer need? The pilot succeeds when it produces evidence that answers this question affirmatively.
- **Token set clarity** — the essay explicitly defines the intended token set: $USDC (funding), $CITY (engagement/civic credits), $VOTE (governance). This three-token architecture is the language of municipal finance: stable funding, recognition currency, governance rights. Each token serves a distinct institutional function.
