## Full Text

While the vision for City/Sync is extremely ambitious, we recognize the need to start somewhere practical. For us, the ability to run a pilot is a stepping stone toward that vision, and it serves as a procedural artifact for how to replicate the foundational processes that will enable it. The engine of the City/Sync framework relies on the ability to recognize existing communal behaviors and to incentivize new ones. The U.N. estimates that we have approximately 2.1 BILLION people who volunteer monthly either through incorporated entities or through direct volunteerism with each other. This is a massive labor market with no standardized mechanisms that are able to recognize or reward their contributions, or to improve the effectiveness of their coordination efforts. City/Sync wants to prove that through the use of blockchain technologies, we can create a Civic-Labor Credit system that can become a new standard for a Public-Sector economy. This is the Pilot we seek to implement.

## The Three Roles

### Issuer Organizations
Organizations that issue tasks which expand the impact of their organizational mandates — organizations that directly benefit from expanding their functions as a means to execute on their mission statements.

**Eligibility requirements:**
- Must be an incorporated public-service organization
- Must have a local service history
- Must have the capacity to manage a volunteer program
- Ideally already facilitates volunteer work with a dedicated volunteer base

**Governance — Representative Issuer Committee:**
- **Task Rules** — Tasks cannot replace existing paid functions of the Issuer organization. Tasks must facilitate the delivery of a public good or public service.
- **Task Catalog** — Approved list of tasks detailing scope (time, nature, benefit, success criteria), organized into tiers with relative pricing. Establishes consistency across Issuers, predictable credit supply for public planning. Handles task credential requirements (elder care, specializations) and reserves categories for equity/inclusion initiatives.
- **Issuer Onboarding** — Establishes process for onboarding, creates governance agreements among new members.
- **Issuance Cap** — Maximum credits distributable in a given time period. The primary economic stability control tool.

### Redeemer Organizations
Organizations that offer access to goods and services in exchange for civic credit redemption. Typically already provide public services or public goods as part of their mission, including common goods and club goods (transit, museums, zoos, etc.).

**Key insight:** Redeemer Organizations typically receive subsidies for providing these goods already, so granting access does not create additional financial dependence. Organizations with unused capacity (empty bus seats, museum off-peak hours) are ideal.

**Governance — Representative Redeemer Committee:**
- **Redemption Rules** — Processes for introducing, modifying, and removing offerings. Emphasis on consistency and stable expectations for Civic Participants. Manages offering timelines and frequency of change.
- **Redemption Rates Guidance** — Each Redeemer has full autonomy over rates and offerings, but the Committee sets credit-redemption targets that help balance the Issuance Rate and expand the Issuance Cap.
- **Redeemer Onboarding** — Process and governance agreements for new members.

### Civic Participants
Individuals who claim and execute civic-labor opportunities and redeem credits with Redeemers. Responsible for increasing execution capacity of the public sector.

**Onboarding:** Required in-person onboarding with a local Issuer Organization to activate mobile wallet.

**Governance — Representative Civic Committee (top participants by Vote balance):**
- **Issuer/Redeemer Feedback** — Aggregates participant feedback on organizations, tasks, redemptions, and network operations into periodic reports.
- **Conflict Resolution** — Handles conflicts within Issuer or Redeemer governance committees (onboarding disputes, issuance conflicts, redemption rule disagreements).

## The Civic-Credit Lifecycle

1. Issuer Organizations submit tasks to the Task Catalog for approval.
2. Once approved, Issuers issue tasks with set credit values.
3. Civic Participants claim tasks and execute.
4. Issuer Organizations verify task completion.
5. Issuer Organizations release Credits to Civic Participants.
6. Every Credit earned receives an equal amount of $VOTE (1:1).
7. Civic Participants exchange Civic Credits for access to goods or services.
8. Redeemer Organizations burn Civic Credits upon redemption.

## Economics of a Civic-Labor Credit System

A CLC system is a **bounded coordination economy** — not a monetary economy. It formalizes civic contribution into an accounting unit minted upon verified civic-labor and extinguished upon redemption.

**Primary objective:** Throughput optimization — increasing volume and efficiency of civic contribution without destabilizing institutional capacity.

### Two Equilibrium Models

**1. Psychological Equilibrium**
The system only works if two things feel true simultaneously:
- It feels possible to earn credits
- It feels possible to use them

*Imbalance 1 (over-issuance):* Tasks abundant, credits flow quickly, but redemption options don't expand. Participants accumulate large balances with limited redemption pathways. Credits feel less meaningful; incentive to participate erodes.

*Imbalance 2 (over-redemption):* Redemption is attractive but earning feels slow. Takes too long to accumulate enough credits to participate meaningfully.

**2. Capacity Equilibrium**
Physical and organizational limits create hard ceilings — only so many bus seats, museum spaces, and legitimate tasks available at any time.

### Key Economic Controls

- **Issuance Cap** — Maximum credits distributable per time period. Functions like interest rates in monetary policy: expands when burn rates are strong, tightens when credits accumulate without use. Should become responsive to real data in a mature system.
- **Task Pricing** — Task Catalog tiers establish relative value of civic contributions.
- **Redemption Rates** — Set by individual Redeemers; Committee sets guidance targets to balance the system.

## The Pilot Program

**Target locations:** Berkeley, California and Mexico City, Mexico
**Purpose:** Validate that programmable coordination can be adopted within varied institutional environments without requiring structural disruption to existing administrative systems.

### Four Objectives
1. **Procedural** — Establish a repeatable municipal onboarding process.
2. **Economic** — Validate participation, issuance, and service redemption mechanisms and processes.
3. **Social** — Validate Mass Coordination Events (MCEs) as a method for bootstrapping local administration.
4. **Governance** — Expand the Issuer and Redeemer ecosystem through real-world operational use.

---

## Mass Coordination Events (MCEs)

A mechanism to be tested within the Pilot Program. After the initial Issuer/Redeemer cohort is onboarded, MCEs are city-wide initiatives that create a visible public benefit using the underlying technical architecture.

**MCEs accomplish two things:**
1. Allow Issuer Organizations to work collaboratively toward a larger public benefit initiative (practicing collective coordination at scale).
2. Incentivize onboarding of new Civic Participants, Issuers, and Redeemers by creating legitimacy.

**Extended redemption universe during MCEs:** Private-sector sponsors (restaurants, grocery stores, coffee shops) can temporarily honor unique credits issued for MCE tasks — illustrating the power of networked coordination and creating strong participation incentives.

**Strategic purpose:** Legitimacy engine. If hundreds of tasks are issued and executed by hundreds of citizens around a collective community need, it validates the framework for prospective organizations, cities, and governments.
